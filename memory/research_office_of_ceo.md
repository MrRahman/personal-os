# Research: Office of the CEO Models

**Date**: 2026-04-14
**Context**: Research for Sulaiman Rahman — exploring OCEO models at high-growth technology and financial services companies, with applicability to a diversified crypto/payments/treasury company (Ripple-like context).

---

## 1. What Does a Typical Office of the CEO Look Like?

### Structure and Team Size

The Office of the CEO (OCEO) is not a single role — it is an **organizational unit** that wraps around the CEO to multiply their effectiveness. The size and composition vary significantly:

**Small OCEO (3-5 people):**
- Chief of Staff
- 1-2 Executive Assistants
- Possibly a Strategy/Operations associate
- Common at companies $500M-$2B revenue, or pre-IPO high-growth

**Medium OCEO (6-12 people):**
- Chief of Staff (who manages the office)
- Deputy Chief of Staff or Director of Strategy & Operations
- Executive Assistants (1-2)
- Corporate Strategy lead
- Communications/Narrative lead
- Special projects associate(s)
- Common at public companies or multi-business-line organizations

**Large OCEO (12+ people):**
- All of the above, plus dedicated heads of Legal (GC office), Investor Relations, Government Affairs, Corporate Development
- Goldman Sachs model: the "Executive Office" is a full division encompassing corporate strategy, client engagement, alumni relations, government affairs, and public policy
- Common at financial institutions, conglomerates, holding companies

**Key statistic**: Only ~15% of surveyed companies have a formal Office of the CEO. They are more common at: (1) government agencies, (2) holding companies with complex portfolios, and (3) businesses undergoing major organizational change.

### Reporting Lines

Two primary models exist:

**Model A — Flat to CEO**: Both the Chief of Staff and EA(s) report directly to the CEO. Works when the team is small (2-3 people). Simpler, but the CEO has more direct reports.

**Model B — CoS as OCEO Head**: The Chief of Staff manages the office. EAs, strategy associates, and communications staff report to the CoS. The CoS is the single point of accountability to the CEO. This is more scalable and is the dominant model at companies like GitLab, LinkedIn, and Goldman Sachs.

At GitLab (one of the most transparent examples due to their open handbook): the Chief of Staff to the CEO serves as Head of the Office of the CEO, with a Director of Strategy and Operations reporting to them. The S&O team functions as "internal strategy consultants" who move from problem to problem across functional areas.

### How It Differs from a Traditional Chief of Staff or Strategy Team

The core distinction:

| | Chief of Staff | Office of the CEO | Strategy Team |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What it gives you** | Leverage (one person) | Structure (a system) | Analysis (a function) |
| **Scope** | CEO effectiveness | CEO + organizational alignment | Strategy formulation |
| **Authority** | Borrowed from CEO | Structural + borrowed | Functional |
| **Orientation** | Inward (exec team ops) | 360 (internal + external) | Analytical |
| **Duration** | Often 2-3 year tours | Permanent infrastructure | Permanent function |
| **Reports to** | CEO directly | CEO (via CoS) | Often CFO or CSO |

The Chief of Staff is typically *a role within* the OCEO. The OCEO is the *system* — it includes the CoS but also the communications, strategy, and administrative infrastructure that makes the CEO effective.

## 2. Common Focus Areas

### A. Strategic Intelligence / Competitive Intelligence

- The OCEO often houses or coordinates competitive intelligence because the CEO needs a **synthesized, cross-functional view** that no single business line can produce
- Goldman Sachs' Executive Office includes a "Client Strategy Group" that works with executives to prioritize and organize interactions — essentially a strategic intelligence function for client relationships
- In practice: the OCEO curates a weekly or biweekly strategic intelligence brief for the CEO, pulling from market data, competitor moves, regulatory shifts, and internal data. The CoS or a dedicated strategist triages what reaches the CEO
- For a company like Ripple (crypto + payments + treasury + prime brokerage): the OCEO would synthesize competitive moves across each vertical — not waiting for each BU head to surface threats in their QBR

### B. CEO Operating Cadence Management

This is the **most universally acknowledged** OCEO function. The Chief of Staff owns the operating rhythm:

**HashiCorp model** (well-documented):
- **Annual**: 12 scorecards with OKRs, set the year's direction
- **Quarterly**: QBRs reviewing scorecard progress, using WAR tables (Wins, Action Items, Requests for Support)
- **Weekly**: Executive staff meetings reviewing key metrics and high-leverage actions
- Principles: federated accountability, reflection over perfection, finding leverage

**LinkedIn model** (Brian Rumao, CoS to Jeff Weiner for 6 years):
- Introduced QBRs in Q1 2015 — leaders of 7 business lines presented to exec team for 1-3 hours over 2 days
- Schedule provided 2 months in advance; materials due 24-48 hours before
- The CoS managed strategic initiatives, the "operating rhythm of the business," cross-functional programs, and leadership communications
- Called it "transformative" — QBRs became the backbone of LinkedIn's operating system

**Typical cadence architecture owned by OCEO:**
- **Weekly**: Executive staff meeting (updates + 1-2 spotlight topics curated by CoS)
- **Monthly**: Business reviews / metrics deep-dives
- **Quarterly**: QBRs per business line + OKR grading + leadership offsites
- **Annual**: Strategic planning cycle + board offsite
- **Ad hoc**: Crisis coordination, M&A war rooms

### C. Cross-Functional Coordination / Breaking Down Silos

This is where the OCEO earns its keep — or becomes "the CEO's police" (see failure modes below).

**Why the OCEO is positioned for this:**
- The OCEO has a **bird's-eye view** across all functions because it sees the CEO's full information flow
- Companies with strong cross-functional alignment grow revenue 58% faster and are 72% more profitable (CIO.com research)
- 78% of organizations struggle with breaking down structural barriers between technical teams
- Microsoft's turnaround under Nadella explicitly required breaking internal silos — this was driven from the CEO's office, not from any single function

**In practice:**
- The OCEO identifies "stuck" cross-functional initiatives and escalates or coordinates
- The CoS designs the exec staff meeting structure to force cross-functional visibility
- Special projects that span multiple BUs are staffed and tracked from the OCEO
- For a multi-product company (e.g., Ripple with crypto, payments, treasury, prime brokerage): the OCEO ensures the CEO's priorities cascade consistently across BUs and that inter-BU dependencies are tracked

### D. M&A Integration Oversight

- The Integration Management Office (IMO) is typically a dedicated cross-functional team, but **governance** often runs through the OCEO
- The CEO creates a steering committee that oversees the IMO — the CoS usually staffs and runs that steering committee
- BCG's 2024 M&A report emphasizes that M&A must report to C-level to ensure strategic alignment — the OCEO is the natural home for this oversight
- Common failure: acquirers create "destructive complexity" when bringing small assets together with no clear integration plan. The OCEO prevents this by maintaining the strategic rationale and holding BU leaders accountable to integration milestones
- For serial acquirers: the OCEO develops a **repeatable integration playbook** — not reinventing the wheel each time

### E. Corporate Narrative / Storytelling Alignment

McKinsey's research identifies the CEO as "Chief Storyteller" — responsible for articulating: **Who are we, why do we exist, what do we want to achieve, and when will we share our plans.**

**The OCEO's role in narrative:**
- Ensuring the "four W's" are consistent across all channels: investor calls, all-hands, press, customer conversations, regulatory engagement
- Goldman Sachs' Executive Office is explicitly responsible for "preserving the firm's distinctive culture" and managing relationships with alumni, clients, shareholders, policymakers
- The "holy grail" (per McKinsey): 12 people on a management team who are **equal storytellers** — they can speak for the company, not just their function. The OCEO coaches this consistency
- Brian Rumao's LinkedIn role explicitly included "leadership communications and company narratives"
- For Ripple context: with business lines spanning crypto infrastructure, payments, treasury, and prime brokerage — narrative coherence is existential. Each BU could tell a different story. The OCEO ensures one strategic narrative with BU-specific proof points

### F. External Engagement Strategy for the CEO

McKinsey's CEO Excellence framework identifies CEO stakeholder management as one of six core responsibilities. The best CEOs:
- Systematically prioritize stakeholder interactions
- Take an "expanded view of the CEO's role as the company's bridge to the external world"
- Build a "growth-oriented mindset that empowers internal and external ambassadors"

**OCEO role in practice:**
- Manages the CEO's external calendar to ensure it maps to strategic priorities (not just whoever asks loudest)
- Curates conference appearances, media opportunities, regulator meetings, and key customer relationships
- Goldman Sachs has a dedicated "Client Strategy Group" within the Executive Office for this
- The CoS serves as advance team: pre-briefing the CEO, debriefing after, and ensuring follow-ups happen

## 3. Authority Model: How OCEOs Get Authority

### Borrowed Authority vs. Structural Authority

The OCEO operates on a **hybrid authority model** — and getting this right is the difference between effectiveness and organizational resentment.

**Borrowed Authority**: The CoS and OCEO team act with the CEO's implicit backing. When the CoS says "the CEO needs this by Friday," that carries weight — not because of the CoS's title, but because of the CEO's shadow. Research shows: "The more prominently the CEO advocates for [the office] and reinforces imperatives, the stronger the halo-effect and the greater the influence."

**Structural Authority**: The OCEO owns processes (the QBR cadence, the board prep process, the strategic planning cycle). This gives it legitimate process authority even without invoking the CEO's name.

**The critical balance:**

| Authority Type | When It Works | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Borrowed (CEO says) | Urgent unblocking, crisis response | Overuse creates resentment — "the CEO's police" |
| Structural (process says) | Cadence management, information flow | Can become bureaucratic if over-designed |
| Relational (trust says) | Cross-functional coordination, influencing peers | Takes time to build; fragile to CoS turnover |

### How to Avoid Being "The CEO's Police"

This is the single most common anti-pattern. Executives without strong borrowed authority report spending **~50% of their time justifying their existence** instead of doing productive work.

**What works:**
1. **Serve, don't direct**: The OCEO succeeds when it helps other leaders be effective — not when it tells them what to do. The CoS is an "air traffic controller," not a cop
2. **Transparent mandate**: The CEO must publicly articulate *why* the OCEO exists and what it does. Ambiguity breeds suspicion
3. **Relationship-first**: The CoS must invest heavily in 1:1 relationships with each exec team member. Trust is the operating system
4. **Credit flows outward**: The OCEO gets no credit. All wins belong to the functional leaders. The moment the OCEO starts taking credit, it becomes a political threat
5. **Information broker, not gatekeeper**: The OCEO should increase information flow, not restrict access to the CEO
6. **Tour of duty model**: Many companies rotate CoS roles every 2-3 years specifically to prevent power accumulation

## 4. Success Patterns and Failure Modes

### What Makes OCEOs Work Well

1. **CEO buy-in is non-negotiable**: McKinsey's research: "The most successful CEOs quickly establish an office (often including one or two highly skilled EAs and a chief of staff) that makes their priorities explicit and helps them spend their scarce time doing work that only CEOs can do." The CEO must *want* this and *use* it — not just tolerate it.

2. **The CoS is a "force multiplier," not a gatekeeper**: Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong described the CoS role as a "force multiplier for product and operations." The CoS enables the CEO to focus on high-level strategy by absorbing coordination and follow-through work.

3. **Clear swim lanes**: The OCEO must have crisp boundaries with the COO, CFO, and CHRO. The OCEO handles CEO effectiveness and cross-functional alignment; the COO handles operational execution; the CFO handles financial stewardship.

4. **Process ownership with light touch**: The OCEO owns *how* the exec team works together (cadence, information flow, decision-making hygiene) but not *what* each function does.

5. **High-caliber talent on short rotations**: The best OCEOs attract top talent by offering 2-3 year "tours of duty" that serve as leadership accelerators. GitLab notes that OCEO alumni move into leadership, corporate strategy, operations management, and communications roles.

6. **The "first 10 systems" mindset** (First Round Review / Kevin Fishner of HashiCorp): Building systems (operating cadence, decision-making frameworks, communication norms) matters more than individual heroics. The OCEO is the system-builder.

### Common Failure Modes

1. **"CEO's police" syndrome**: The OCEO over-invokes the CEO's name, creates a fear dynamic, and other executives route around it. Symptoms: people stop sharing bad news with the OCEO, executives complain to the board.

2. **Scope creep into operations**: The OCEO starts making operational decisions that belong to functional leaders. This undermines accountability and creates confusion about who owns what.

3. **Information hoarding / gatekeeping**: The CoS becomes the bottleneck for CEO access. Other executives feel they need "permission" to talk to the CEO. This is toxic and fast-acting.

4. **Shadow power structure**: The OCEO becomes an informal power center that undermines the formal org chart. Decisions get made in hallways between the CoS and CEO, bypassing the exec team. Research shows: "The formal org chart represents authority but not necessarily influence — the actual power structure flows beneath it through informal networks."

5. **Talent mismatch**: Hiring someone too junior (treats it as an admin role) or too senior (creates turf wars with other C-suite). The ideal CoS is 1-2 levels below C-suite in capability, with strong EQ and zero ego.

6. **No clear mandate**: The CEO doesn't publicly explain what the OCEO does. Other leaders project their fears onto it. Ambiguity is the enemy.

7. **Permanence without refresh**: The same CoS stays 5+ years and becomes part of the furniture — or worse, a political player. The tour-of-duty model prevents this.

## 5. Known Examples

### Goldman Sachs — "Executive Office" Division

- A full **front-office division** (not a staff function) that sets and advances corporate strategy while preserving the firm's culture
- Encompasses: corporate strategy, Client Strategy Group, Office of Corporate Engagement, Office of Government and Regulatory Affairs, alumni relations, investor/shareholder engagement, public policy
- Reports to the Chairman/CEO (David Solomon) through the President/COO (John Waldron)
- The "Office of the Chairman" specifically oversees internal and external strategic initiatives, communications, and investor/government/community/client engagement
- This is the most expansive OCEO model in financial services — it functions as the firm's strategic brain and external engagement engine

### GitLab — Fully Documented Open Handbook

- Chief of Staff to the CEO serves as **Head of the Office of the CEO**
- Director of Strategy and Operations reports to the CoS
- S&O team functions as "internal strategy consultants" moving from problem to problem
- Responsibilities documented publicly: board prep, special projects, multi-functional initiatives, CEO communications, investor updates, presentations
- Includes both "directed work" (CEO-triggered) and "leadership work" (self-directed strategic projects)
- Perhaps the best public documentation of an OCEO in existence

### LinkedIn — Brian Rumao Model

- Brian Rumao served as VP/Chief of Staff to CEO Jeff Weiner for 6+ years
- Owned: strategic initiatives, operating rhythm, cross-functional programs, leadership communications, company narratives
- Designed and launched the QBR process that became LinkedIn's operating backbone
- Published extensively on the model — now a thought leader on CoS practice
- Key insight: the CoS role at LinkedIn was explicitly about **operating the business system**, not just supporting the CEO personally

### Block (Jack Dorsey) — Functional Reorganization Model

- In 2024, Dorsey reorganized Block by **disbanding business unit structure** entirely, grouping employees by function (engineering, design, sales) instead
- This is relevant because it shows what happens when cross-BU coordination fails: the CEO eventually blows up the BU model
- Block's leadership structure includes a Business Lead, Engineering Lead, Sales & Marketing Lead — all reporting to Dorsey
- The 2024 reorg was explicitly about breaking down silos that had calcified between Square, Cash App, and other lines
- Lesson: if the OCEO doesn't successfully coordinate across BUs, the CEO may resort to structural demolition

### Coinbase — CoS as "Force Multiplier"

- Hired Chief of Staff to President Emilie Choi — role described as "integrator across product, markets, and corporate development"
- CEO Brian Armstrong called it a "force multiplier" for product and operations
- Focus: program management, cross-team coordination, strategic prioritization
- Signals: tightening governance and execution as the company matures post-IPO

### HashiCorp — Operating Cadence Blueprint

- While not specifically an "OCEO" structure, HashiCorp's documented operating cadence (annual scorecards, quarterly QBRs with WAR tables, weekly exec meetings) represents the **process infrastructure** that an OCEO typically builds and maintains
- Principles: federated accountability, reflection over perfection, finding leverage
- Kevin Fishner (CoS/COO) published the "first 10 systems" framework — build systems, not just hire people

### Stripe — COO Model (Claire Hughes Johnson)

- Stripe chose a COO model over a formal OCEO — Claire Hughes Johnson was COO from 2014-2021
- Hughes Johnson has expressed skepticism about founders hiring chiefs of staff too early, recommending a BizOps lead first
- Relevant because it shows an alternative path: instead of an OCEO, Stripe invested in a strong COO who owned operational scaling
- Her book *Scaling People* documents the operating principles that a CoS/OCEO would otherwise implement

## 6. The "Operating Extension" Framing

### What It Means

Many OCEOs describe themselves as "an operating extension of the CEO." This is the most useful framing because it draws a clear line against two lesser interpretations:

**It is NOT an executive assistant team.** EAs create leverage by absorbing work that fragments the CEO's attention — scheduling, travel, inbox management. This is necessary but operates at the **task level**.

**It is NOT a strategy consulting team.** Strategy teams analyze and recommend. They produce decks. They don't own execution or follow-through.

**An "operating extension" means:**
1. The OCEO **thinks with** the CEO — serving as a sounding board, stress-testing ideas, providing alternative perspectives before decisions go to the exec team
2. The OCEO **sees for** the CEO — scanning the organization, surfacing information the CEO would otherwise miss, acting as an early warning system
3. The OCEO **acts for** the CEO — following up on commitments, coordinating across functions, preparing people and materials for the CEO's moments of highest leverage
4. The OCEO **protects** the CEO's time — ruthlessly prioritizing what reaches the CEO, ensuring the CEO spends time on things only the CEO can do

### How This Plays Out in Practice (vs. EA Team)

| Dimension | EA Team | OCEO as Operating Extension |
|---|---|---|
| **Calendar** | Manages logistics | Manages **strategic allocation** of CEO time |
| **Meetings** | Schedules them | Designs the content, ensures pre-reads, drives follow-ups |
| **Information** | Routes messages | Curates, synthesizes, and contextualizes information |
| **Decisions** | None | Prepares decision frameworks, ensures the right people are in the room |
| **Follow-through** | None | Owns accountability tracking for CEO-level commitments |
| **Narrative** | None | Ensures consistency of message across all channels |
| **External** | Books travel | Manages strategic stakeholder relationship cadence |

### The Maturity Curve

A common mistake: CEOs hire an EA and think they have an "operating extension." The typical maturity progression:

1. **Stage 1**: EA manages logistics (solo CEO, <50 people)
2. **Stage 2**: EA + BizOps lead manage operations and early cadences (50-200 people)
3. **Stage 3**: Chief of Staff owns operating rhythm, special projects, exec team alignment (200-1000 people)
4. **Stage 4**: Formal Office of the CEO with CoS, S&O team, comms, and possibly strategy (1000+ people or multi-BU)

Claire Hughes Johnson's advice: don't skip to Stage 3 before you've built Stage 2. Many founders hire a CoS when they actually need a BizOps lead.

## 7. Implications for a Ripple-Like Context

A company with crypto infrastructure, cross-border payments, treasury/custody, and prime brokerage — going through rapid growth, multiple acquisitions, product line diversification, and regulatory complexity — has several characteristics that make an OCEO particularly valuable:

1. **Multiple business lines with different market dynamics** demand cross-BU coordination that no single BU head can provide. Without it, you get the Block problem (silos so deep the CEO has to restructure the entire company)

2. **Serial M&A** requires a permanent integration oversight function — not ad hoc project teams that disband after each deal. The OCEO can own the integration playbook and steering committee

3. **Regulatory complexity across jurisdictions** means the CEO's external engagement calendar is a strategic asset. The OCEO should manage regulator relationships, government affairs, and conference strategy as a coordinated portfolio

4. **Narrative coherence** is existential when you have crypto-native, TradFi, and institutional audiences. Each BU could tell a different story. The OCEO ensures one strategic narrative with audience-specific proof points

5. **Operating cadence** becomes critical as the org scales past the point where the CEO can personally track every initiative. The QBR/weekly exec/offsite architecture is the OCEO's primary infrastructure deliverable

6. **CEO time is the scarcest resource** — with investors, regulators, customers, partners, media, and internal stakeholders all competing for attention. The OCEO is the allocation engine
